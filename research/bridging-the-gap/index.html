The PrEP Cascade Paradigm Shift

A visual walkthrough of how LAI-PrEP inverts the prevention cascade — shifting the critical failure point from post-initiation adherence to pre-initiation structural barriers, and why the 2–8 week bridge period is the key to unlocking global impact.

Slide 01 — Title
Bridging the Gap: The PrEP Cascade Paradigm Shift for Long-Acting Injectable HIV Prevention. Converting clinical efficacy into public health impact. Data sources: HPTN 083/084, PURPOSE-1/2, CAN Community Health Network.
Slide 02 — From Human to System Perfection
The fundamental shift: from human perfection to system perfection. Oral PrEP demands daily behavioral compliance. LAI-PrEP transfers the burden to structural system dependency — clinical integration, scheduled dosings, and system adherence replace the daily pill decision.
Slide 03 — The Clinical Triumph
The chemistry is solved. The drug works perfectly. Greater than 96% efficacy across all groups (PURPOSE-2). Zero infections in the PURPOSE-1 cohort (n=5,338 cisgender women). 66% superiority over oral PrEP in HPTN 083, 89% in HPTN 084.
Slide 04 — The Implementation Paradox
From 99% efficacy to 53% real-world initiation. The 47.1% pre-initiation attrition rate means nearly half of prescribed patients never receive their first injection. Oral PrEP fails through behavioral barriers (daily pill burden); LAI-PrEP fails through structural barriers (mandatory 2–8 week bridge).
Slide 05 — The Reconceptualized Cascade
The drop-off happens BEFORE the start. The oral PrEP cascade fails at persistence (40–48% lost to daily pill burden). The LAI-PrEP cascade fails at bridge navigation (47.1% lost in the 2–8 week gap). This requires a fundamentally reconceptualized care cascade with bridge period navigation as a distinct, measurable step.
Slide 06 — Anatomy of the Bridge Period
The patient is motivated, but unprotected. From Day 0 (prescription) to Day 60 (first injection), patients must navigate three structural hurdles: insurance authorization and prior auth, HIV testing window periods (RNA vs. Ag/Ab), and cold-chain logistics and scheduling.
Slide 07 — Adolescents (16–24)
Adolescents face unique developmental barriers. Projected completion: 30–40%. Temporal discounting reduces future risk salience. Privacy fears around Explanation of Benefits (EOB) statements deter youth on parental insurance from initiating LAI-PrEP.
Slide 08 — Cisgender Women
Competing priorities and medical mistrust create compounding barriers for cisgender women. Projected completion: 40–50%. Childcare and transportation demands compete with multi-visit bridge requirements. 39% cite side effect concerns. Contrast with PURPOSE-1: zero infections in the trial setting.
Slide 09 — People Who Inject Drugs
PWID face the most severe structural barriers and the lowest projected completion. Projected: 20–30%. Criminalization (17% avoid care due to legal fear), housing instability preventing appointment tracking, and an awareness gap (only 2% PrEP usage despite high need).
Slide 10 — Transgender Persons
The trust gap: healthcare discrimination, economic marginalization, and hormone interaction concerns. Projected completion: 35–45%. These barriers compound to create healthcare avoidance. Contrast with PURPOSE-1: zero infections in the trial setting, where structural barriers were minimized.
Slide 11 — Global Context: Sub-Saharan Africa
62% of global need, magnified structural deficits. Cold chain requirements (lenacapavir at 2–8°C vs. cabotegravir), healthcare workforce shortages, and centralized lab testing creating 2–4 week delays. The bridge period challenge scales proportionally with infrastructure gaps.
Slide 12 — Strategy 1: Eliminate the Bridge
The highest-impact strategy: eliminate the bridge entirely. Oral-to-injectable switch bypasses the bridge period for existing PrEP users. Tier 1 (strong) evidence. Impact: increases initiation to 85–90%. The bridge becomes a non-issue when patients transition from continuous oral coverage.
Slide 13 — Strategy 2: Compress the Bridge
Advanced HIV testing strategies reduce the danger zone by ~30 days. Standard Ag/Ab testing requires a 45-day window period with high attrition risk. RNA/rapid testing compresses this to 10–14 days, enabling rapid start and dramatically reducing the exposure window.
Slide 14 — The POC Challenge
The access vs. safety tradeoff. Lack of point-of-care RNA testing creates multi-visit burdens. WHO July 2025 guidance offers mitigation. In resource-limited settings, the strategy must prioritize access over maximal risk reduction — because attrition from delayed access prevents more infections than the residual window period risk.
Slide 15 — The Intervention Library
21 evidence-based strategies categorized by rigor. Tier 1 (Proven/RCTs): oral switch, patient navigation, SMS reminders. Tier 2 (Observational): RNA testing, transport support. Tier 3 (Emerging): pharmacist prescribing, bundled payments. Source: global health evidence synthesis and WHO guidelines.
Slide 16 — The CDS Tool
Navigating the bridge requires a GPS, not just a map. The clinical decision support algorithm takes a patient profile (e.g., adolescent + no transport) and generates a tailored intervention recommendation (e.g., Tier 1: SMS reminders + Tier 2: rideshare voucher). Validated computationally at 21.2M patients in the companion paper.
Slide 17 — Equity Implications
Interventions narrow the gap. Without them, LAI-PrEP widens inequality. Adolescents: +147% relative improvement. PWID: +265%. The populations facing the greatest structural barriers see the greatest marginal benefit from systematic bridge period support.
Slide 18 — Safety & Irreversibility
The bridge period is a safety feature, not just a barrier. We don’t abandon safety; we streamline the process. The islatravir lesson (monitoring failures) vs. lenacapavir’s success reinforces that safety protocols must be maintained while reducing structural friction.
Slide 19 — Research Priorities
Four research priorities to close the evidence-to-implementation gap. Real-world measurement of bridge duration. Economic evaluation of RNA testing vs. attrition costs. Implementation trials (HPTN 102/103). Equity monitoring through EquiPrEP protocols.
Slide 20 — The New Paradigm
The bridge is the key to impact. From behavioral adherence → structural access. From individual burden → system responsibility. The LAI-PrEP implementation paradox is solvable — but only if we reconceptualize where the failure occurs and invest in the systems to navigate it.
